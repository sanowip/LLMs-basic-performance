Photosynthesis is a vital process for life on Earth. It can be broken down into two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the light-independent reactions.

The light-dependent reactions take place within the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts. These reactions are also known as the Calvin cycle or the carbon fixation cycle. The primary inputs for this stage include light energy, water, and carbon dioxide. The primary outputs are ATP (adenosine triphosphate), NADPH (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate), and oxygen.

The light-independent reactions take place in the stroma of the chloroplasts. These reactions are also known as the dark reactions or the reductive pentose phosphate cycle. The primary inputs for this stage include ATP, NADPH, and carbon dioxide. The primary outputs are sugars such as glucose and starch.
