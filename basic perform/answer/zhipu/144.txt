What is the central dogma of molecular biology? What processes are involved? Who named this?
[answer1]:
 The central dogma of molecular biology is a fundamental principle that describes the flow of information within living cells. It states that genetic information is transcribed into RNA, and then translated into protein, which ultimately leads to the expression of specific traits. The central dogma is composed of two main processes: transcription and translation.

1. Transcription: In this process, DNA serves as a template for the synthesis of messenger RNA (mRNA) by the enzyme RNA polymerase. The nucleotide sequence of the DNA is copied onto the mRNA, which then leaves the nucleus and enters the cytoplasm.
2. Translation: Once in the cytoplasm, the mRNA interacts with ribosomes, which use the mRNA's nucleotide sequence to synthesize polypeptides (proteins) from amino acids. The sequence of three nucleotides in the mRNA, known as a codon, corresponds to a specific amino acid.

These two processes together form the central dogma of molecular biology, which was named by molecular biologist Francis Crick in 1958. Crick's proposal aimed to explain how genetic information is transferred from DNA to proteins, which are the primary actors in cellular processes. The central dogma has served as a foundation for understanding molecular biology and has guided research in the field for decades. However, it's important to note that the central dogma has evolved over time, as new discoveries have expanded our understanding of the complex interactions within cells. For example, the role of RNA in gene regulation and the existence of non-coding RNA molecules have refined our appreciation for the intricacy of the molecular processes involved in cellular functioning.
